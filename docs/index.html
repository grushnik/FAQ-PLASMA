Frequently asked questions about Radom plasma technology

This page provides a concise reference of common questions and answers about plasma and Radom microwave plasma technology.

What is gas plasma?
Gas plasma is an ionized gas formed when energy is applied to a neutral gas, creating a mixture of electrons, ions, neutrals, radicals, and excited species that can conduct electricity and drive chemical reactions. Depending on the energy input, it can be cold (non-thermal), where the ions and neutrals remain near ambient temperature, or hot (thermal), where the entire gas reaches very high temperatures, enabling applications such as material processing, chemical conversion, sterilization, and environmental treatment.
What are examples of plasma?
Common examples of plasma include lightning, auroras, and the Sun and other stars, where gases are naturally ionized at high energy. Man-made examples include neon and fluorescent lights, arc welding plasmas, plasma cutting torches, and microwave or radio-frequency plasma used in laboratories and industry for materials processing and chemical conversion.
How long does plasma live?
Plasma does not have a fixed “lifetime” on its own—it exists only as long as energy is supplied to sustain ionization. When the power is on, plasma can be maintained continuously for seconds, hours, or indefinitely in steady-state operation; when the power is turned off, the plasma typically recombines and disappears in microseconds to milliseconds, depending on pressure, gas composition, and temperature.
Why are plasma torches used to generate plasma?
People create plasma in torches because it provides a highly energetic, controllable environment that can drive reactions and processes impossible or inefficient with conventional heat or chemistry. Plasma torches deliver extreme temperatures, reactive species, and strong electromagnetic coupling, enabling rapid chemical conversion, material synthesis, destruction of persistent contaminants, surface modification, and energy-efficient processing in applications such as waste treatment, gas reforming, materials production, and advanced research.
What type of plasma torches exist?
Plasma torches are commonly classified by how plasma is generated and sustained. Arc plasma torches use an electrical arc between electrodes and are widely used for cutting, welding, and thermal processing. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) torches use radio-frequency electromagnetic fields to generate plasma without electrodes, enabling clean, stable operation for analytical and materials applications. Microwave plasma torches use microwave energy to sustain plasma at atmospheric or reduced pressure and are valued for high efficiency, electrode-free operation, and precise control. DC and AC plasma torches are often used in high-power industrial systems, while low-pressure (cold) plasma sources are used for surface treatment and sterilization.
What’s the difference between cold plasma and hot plasma?
Cold plasma operates at low bulk gas temperatures while maintaining highly energetic electrons, making it well suited for heat-sensitive applications, as cold plasma operates far from thermal equilibrium. In contrast, hot (thermal) plasma operates at very high gas temperatures, typically thousands of Kelvin, and approaches local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE), where electrons, ions, and neutral species all possess high and comparable energies.
What temperature range does microwave plasma usually operate in?
The typical temperature of microwave plasma depends on the operating regime: in thermal (hot) microwave plasma, gas temperatures are usually in the range of 3,000–6,000 K and can exceed 7,000 K in some configurations, while in non-thermal (cold) microwave plasma, electrons are highly energetic but the bulk gas temperature remains near room temperature up to a few hundred degrees Celsius; atmospheric-pressure microwave plasma torches used for materials processing, gas conversion, and waste treatment therefore commonly operate at thousands of Kelvin.
What kinds of gas-phase kinetics occur at the 3,000–6,000 K temperatures typical of thermal microwave plasma?
At 3,000–6,000 K in a thermal (hot) microwave plasma, the gas undergoes extreme kinetic and chemical activity driven by very high particle energies. Molecules rapidly dissociate into atoms, and a significant fraction of atoms become ionized, forming a dense mixture of electrons, ions, radicals, and excited species. Collisions occur on nanosecond timescales, enabling fast reaction kinetics, high diffusion rates, and near-equilibrium chemistry. These conditions allow otherwise stable molecules (such as CO₂, CH₄, or PFAS) to break strong chemical bonds, drive endothermic reactions, and form new species, which is why hot microwave plasma is effective for gas reforming, materials synthesis, and destruction of persistent compounds.
What are typical applications of microwave plasma?
Atmospheric-pressure microwave plasma sources are used for surface treatment, nanoparticle and carbon black production, plasma-activated water (PAW) generation, microbial cleaning, nitrogen fixation and NOx production, PFAS destruction, wastewater treatment, CO₂ conversion, methane reforming, hydrogen production, waste-to-energy and waste-to-X processes, mining waste treatment, gasification, and high-enthalpy plasma research including plasma-assisted propulsion and reentry simulation.
What is CerawaveTM and what difference does it make?
Cerawave™ is Radom’s proprietary ceramic resonator used inside our microwave plasma torches to efficiently couple microwave energy into the plasma. Its unique chemical composition and dielectric properties reduce heat losses while shaping and stabilizing the electromagnetic field around the discharge, improving power transfer, ignition reliability, and plasma stability over a wide operating range. Compared to conventional applicator designs, Cerawave™ enables electrode-free operation, higher efficiency, and more repeatable plasma performance with reduced sensitivity to gas composition and flow variations.
How can plasma be used to produce carbon black?
In thermal plasma, hydrocarbon gases (e.g. methane) are rapidly decomposed at high temperature, forming carbon black nanoparticles and hydrogen.
How does plasma destroy PFAS (and what results can be expected)?
High-temperature plasma breaks strong C–F bonds through thermal dissociation and reactive plasma species, converting PFAS into simpler, non-persistent compounds. Laboratory and pilot results show very high destruction efficiency when operated under appropriate conditions.
Can microwave-based plasma be used for sterilization?
Microwave plasma can be used for sterilization, but hot atmospheric plasma is generally unsuitable for heat-sensitive materials. Low-pressure (cold) plasma may be viable, though it typically requires additional development and validation for regulated sterilization applications.
Can microwave plasma be used for air cleaning or pollutant removal?
Yes. Microwave plasma can break down pollutants, VOCs, and hazardous compounds in air streams through a combination of high temperature and reactive plasma species. The effectiveness depends on gas composition, residence time, and system design; however, nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) may also form during the process and must be managed accordingly.

Argon is used to ignite Radom’s plasma torches because it ionizes easily and provides a stable, low-breakdown-voltage path for plasma initiation. Is a plasma torch safe to operate?
Plasma torch operation is safe when appropriate precautions are followed. The area around the plasma jet should be secured, and personnel should keep clear of the torch head during ignition due to high voltage. An exhaust system is required during operation, and UV-rated safety glasses must be worn whenever the plasma is active. Fire safety and high-voltage precautions should comply with standard industrial safety requirements.
Why is an exhaust system required?
When plasma is operated in air, nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) can be generated at elevated concentrations. These gases are hazardous to humans and corrosive to equipment, making proper ventilation and exhaust essential during operation.
